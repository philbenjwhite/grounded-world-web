As the fashion industry grapples with urgent climate-related concerns, circular fashion emerges as a transformative solution that prioritizes sustainability at every stage of a garment's lifecycle. From reducing textile waste to emphasizing resource efficiency, circularity is more than a trend—it's a necessity for brands looking to not only comply with regulatory pressures but also develop a competitive advantage by taking a leadership position that commercializes sustainability.

## What is circular fashion, and how can it reduce textile waste in the fashion industry?

Circular fashion is a revolutionary approach to the fashion industry that seeks to redefine the way clothing is designed, produced, consumed, and discarded. At its core, circular fashion is an [application of the circular economy](https://grounded.world/going-circular-the-story-of-decathlon/) to the fashion industry, aiming to create a closed-loop system where clothing items are designed, produced, used, and then recycled or repurposed.

The goal of circular fashion is to minimize waste and reduce the environmental impact of the fashion industry, which is currently one of the largest polluters in the world. The traditional linear model of take-make-use-and-dispose is no longer sustainable, and circular fashion offers a new way forward. Redesigning fashion's future, as outlined in the Ellen MacArthur Foundation's report, emphasizes the transition from a linear to a circular model by fostering innovative business practices and sustainability strategies.

Circular fashion is built upon several foundational principles that drive a more sustainable industry:

### Design for Longevity

Garments should be made to last, using high-quality, durable materials and timeless designs that transcend seasonal trends. Brands are increasingly adopting repairable and modular clothing, allowing for easy maintenance and upgrades instead of disposal. Integrating eco-friendly materials into garment production further supports these sustainable practices by reducing waste and environmental impact.

### Reuse and Resale

The rise of second-hand markets, rental services, and peer-to-peer fashion exchanges has extended the life cycle of clothing, preventing it from ending up in landfills prematurely. Innovative business models, such as clothing rental, repair, and resale, help minimize environmental impact and offer opportunities for revenue generation and economic growth within the industry. Companies like The RealReal and ThredUp have capitalized on this growing shift in consumer behavior. Additionally, many fashion brands are now being rated based on their sustainability and social responsibility, encouraging ethical consumption and environmental considerations within the fashion industry.

### Recycling and Upcycling

Circular fashion promotes innovative recycling techniques, such as fiber-to-fiber recycling, where discarded textiles are broken down and transformed into new materials. Upcycling, on the other hand, involves creatively repurposing old garments into fresh designs, reducing waste and encouraging resource efficiency. Circular textiles play a crucial role in creating closed-loop systems that minimize waste and promote recycling and repurposing of materials.

### Sustainable Production

Ethical sourcing of biodegradable, regenerative, or recycled fibers ensures that clothing leaves a minimal environmental footprint. Eliminating toxic dyes, chemicals, and excessive water use further supports a sustainable supply chain. Adopting these practices helps address waste and environmental impact by extending product life cycles, recycling materials, and promoting sustainable methods.

### Closed-loop systems

The ultimate [goal of circular fashion is to create a self-sustaining ecosystem](https://grounded.world/sustainable-fashion-4-ways-fashion-brands-can-be-more-sustainable/) where materials are continually repurposed, waste is minimized, and fashion operates within planetary boundaries.

The importance of circularity cannot be overstated. The fashion industry is responsible for 92 million tons of textile waste annually, much of which could be avoided through better design, reuse models, and material innovation. Additionally, the production of textiles accounts for a significant portion of global carbon emissions and water consumption. Transitioning to circular models is not only an environmental necessity but also a business opportunity, as consumers increasingly demand sustainable options. Fostering collaborations across different sectors can reduce waste and enhance the distribution of value throughout the entire value chain, ultimately leading to more sustainable manufacturing practices.

## Next-Gen Materials: Revolutionizing Fashion with Circularity

The future of fashion lies in innovation, and next-generation materials are leading the way in redefining sustainability. These advanced materials are designed to minimize environmental impact while maintaining high performance and aesthetic appeal. Some of the most promising innovations include:

### Biodegradable & Regenerative Materials

**Bananatex®** – The world’s first durable, biodegradable, and plastic-free fabric made entirely from regeneratively grown Abacá banana plants.

**Keel Labs** – Developing renewable, durable yarns made from kelp, a fast-growing marine plant that requires no freshwater or pesticides.

**BioFluff** – A plant-based alternative to animal fur, created using nettle, hemp, and flax, half of which is sourced from agricultural waste streams.

**AMSilk** – Producing bioengineered silk from man-made proteins, offering sustainable alternatives for textiles, coatings, hydrogels, and medical applications.

**Coreva** – The first plastic-free stretch denim fabric, using plant-based yarns instead of synthetic elastic fibers.

**FLWRDWN™** – A sustainable down-fill alternative made from a blend of wildflowers, a biopolymer, and aerogel, replacing animal-derived down.

**MycoWorks** – Pioneering Fine Mycelium™ technology, which enables the production of premium, non-animal leather alternatives using fungi.

**Oxman** – Producing fully biodegradable textiles and wearables using biofabrication techniques.

**EVO® by FULGAR** – A bio-based yarn derived from castor oil and industrial corn, offering a renewable alternative to petroleum-based fibers.

**Vegetable Cashmere™** – A plant-based fiber blend developed to mimic the softness and warmth of traditional cashmere without relying on animal farming.

### Recycled & Circular Fibers

**ECONYL®** – A regenerated nylon made entirely from ocean and landfill waste, including fishing nets and fabric scraps. This infinitely recyclable material is used by over 1,700 brands worldwide, significantly reducing dependency on virgin petroleum-based nylon.

**loopamid®** – The first fully closed-loop Nylon 6 fiber, transforming post-consumer textile waste into virgin-quality material without downcycling, ensuring true circularity.

**Ioncell®** – Upcycling cellulosic textile waste with bacterial cellulose via an advanced spinning process, offering a sustainable alternative to conventional viscose and cotton fibers.

**Seawool®** – A revolutionary textile blending discarded oyster shells and recycled plastic waste into high-performance, eco-friendly fabrics, reducing both ocean waste and reliance on virgin polyester.

## Circular Fashion Business Models: Rethinking Fashion Consumption

**Nudie Jeans** – A frontrunner in circular fashion, offering free repairs, take-back programs, and recycled denim initiatives to extend the lifecycle of garments.

**eBay's Circular Fashion Fund**– eBay is helping to create a more sustainable fashion ecosystem by promoting rental models and repair solutions that extend the life of garments, preventing waste and reducing the industry's reliance on new production.

**ThredUp & Vestiaire Collective** – Online resale platforms enabling consumers to buy and sell second-hand fashion, slowing down the fast fashion cycle and promoting reuse.

**Mud Jeans**– Mud Jeans offers a leasing model where customers can rent jeans and return them for recycling, promoting a circular economy in fashion.

## Best-in-Class Case Studies of Leading Brands in Circular Fashion

Beyond material innovation, true circularity requires reimagining how fashion is produced, consumed, and disposed of. Several brands are pioneering business models that keep garments in circulation longer and minimize waste: 

[![](/images/blog/2025-02-screen-shot-2025-02-19-at-12.41.53-pm.png)](/images/blog/2025-02-screen-shot-2025-02-19-at-12.41.53-pm.png)

### Prada Re-Nylon Collection

Prada’s Re-Nylon collection utilizes recycled nylon, eliminating the need for new resources while maintaining the luxury and quality for which it is known. Since July 2023, a portion of proceeds from the Re-Nylon for SEA BEYOND collection benefits the SEA BEYOND initiative, showing the brand’s integration of sustainability into its core business model.

 

[![](/images/blog/2025-02-screen-shot-2025-02-19-at-12.45.53-pm.png)](/images/blog/2025-02-screen-shot-2025-02-19-at-12.45.53-pm.png)

### Lululemon and Samsara Eco Partnership 

Lululemon’s partnership with Samsara Eco focuses on enzymatically recycling nylon 6,6 from end-of-life textiles, showcasing a breakthrough in circularity for the apparel industry. The project demonstrates the potential for a fully circular ecosystem that can help reduce waste and lower the environmental impact of clothing production.

 

[![](/images/blog/2025-02-screen-shot-2025-02-19-at-12.48.49-pm.png)](/images/blog/2025-02-screen-shot-2025-02-19-at-12.48.49-pm.png)

### Nike’s Reuse-A-Shoe Program 

Nike’s Reuse-A-Shoe program has been pioneering in recycling used shoes and repurposing them into new products. Since its launch, over 30 million pairs of shoes have been salvaged, marking a significant achievement in the brand’s journey toward circularity. The program not only diverts waste from landfills but also leads to the creation of innovative new products from recycled materials.

 

[![](/images/blog/2025-02-screen-shot-2025-02-19-at-12.50.26-pm.png)](/images/blog/2025-02-screen-shot-2025-02-19-at-12.50.26-pm.png)

### Adidas and Parley for the Oceans 

Adidas has made a bold statement with its Parley for the Oceans initiative, using recycled ocean plastic to create footwear and apparel. With over 30 million pairs of shoes made from ocean plastic, Adidas has become a leader in integrating circular principles into mainstream fashion production. The partnership reflects a successful model of how fashion can align itself with environmental sustainability goals.

 

[![](/images/blog/2025-02-screen-shot-2025-02-19-at-12.51.46-pm.png)](/images/blog/2025-02-screen-shot-2025-02-19-at-12.51.46-pm.png)

### Patagonia’s NetPlus® Recycled Fishing Nets 

Patagonia’s NetPlus® initiative, developed in collaboration with Bureo, upcycles discarded fishing nets into 100% recycled materials used in its products. This project not only prevents plastic from entering the ocean but also supports coastal communities by providing them with alternative income. Through this initiative, Patagonia has recycled over 1,700 metric tons of discarded fishing nets and integrated them into a wide range of products.

 

 

![Zara and BASF create a new circular project](/images/blog/www-the-spin-off-com-zara-and-basf-collaborate-on-circularity-76890.png)

### Zara’s Collaboration with BASF and Circ® 

Zara has taken bold steps in circular fashion with its collaboration with BASF, using Loopamid, a material made entirely from 100% textile waste, to create a jacket composed of 100% recycled polyamide. Additionally, Zara’s partnership with Circ® has led to the development of a collection made from Circ Lyocell, derived from 50% recycled textile waste. This innovative recycling process aims to break down blended fabrics (like polyester and cotton) into usable fibers for new garments, advancing Zara’s sustainability goals.

These examples not only showcase how circularity is integrated into fashion products but also highlight the variety of materials, partnerships, and processes used to achieve sustainability. Each brand plays a unique role in advancing the industry toward circular practices and demonstrating the viability of sustainable fashion.

With legislation tightening, [consumer expectations shifting](https://grounded.world/avoid-greenwashing/), and material innovation accelerating, the brands that act now will define the future of fashion.
